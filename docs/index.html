Anti-Obesity Medications

Off-label AOMs are not FDA-approved specifically for weight loss but have clinical experience and data supporting use. Follow your state's prescribing guidelines. Sources: OMA Algorithm 2025 + Frederiksen lecture.

Top takeaways from the PDF

Interpret cautiously
The algorithm warns that most reported weight effects of concomitant medications come from mean changes in studies not designed primarily to compare body-weight effects head-to-head. Individual response varies a lot.
Clinical use
When weight gain is unexpected or progress stalls, review the med list before assuming “nonadherence.” The drug list itself may be part of the disease burden.

High-yield special notes

Metformin is framed very favorably in the PDF: it may help insulin resistance, PCOS, CVD risk relative to sulfonylureas, and antipsychotic-related weight gain, with roughly 2% average weight loss in randomized trials and 5% in nonrandomized studies.
SGLT2 inhibitors can modestly reduce weight, but the PDF specifically warns about euglycemic ketoacidosis risk, especially when combined with carbohydrate restriction, fasting, or relative insulin deficiency.

Workflow use

1. Review
Scan antihypertensives, diabetes meds, psychotropics, hormones, migraine meds, pain meds, and sleep meds whenever weight gain seems disproportionate.
2. Substitute
When clinically safe, swap toward more weight-neutral or weight-lowering options instead of simply layering on another obesity treatment.
3. Reassess
Interpret weight changes in context: edema, psychiatric indication, organ transplant status, and bariatric history all affect how these medications behave.
The later PDF pages also flag immunosuppressants and transplant medications as contributors to post-transplant metabolic disease. That matters because obesity both worsens transplant risk and is often worsened by the medications used afterward.

This pass also folds in 2025 algorithm update themes highlighted by OMA: telehealth workflows, obesity medicine practice setup, organ transplantation, kidney stones, and orthopedic complications.
  • Treat to disease targets (metabolic disease, fat mass disease), not to a BMI goal alone. Even 5–10% weight loss meaningfully improves adiposopathy and comorbidities.
  • Only 20% of patients respond long-term to lifestyle intervention alone, due to metabolic adaptation: leptin falls, ghrelin rises, thyroid hormone drops, resting metabolic rate decreases. Most patients need pharmacotherapy.
  • AOMs are used by <4% of those who qualify. The biggest gap in obesity medicine is undertreatment, not overtreatment.
  • Obesity is chronic and relapsing — plan for indefinite therapy in most patients. Weight regain after semaglutide discontinuation is rapid and nearly complete within 1 year (STEP 1 extension).
  • Switch or discontinue any AOM if total body weight loss is <3–5% at 3 months despite maximum tolerated dose. Don't persist with a non-responder.
  • Before starting phentermine: consider an EKG (atrial fibrillation is a contraindication). Consider a consent form for long-term off-label use. Document low CV risk in the chart.
  • Phentermine cannot be prescribed via telemedicine in most states (DEA Schedule IV). For telehealth patients: naltrexone/bupropion (unscheduled) or GLP-1 RAs are first-line options.
  • Diethylpropion lasts ~4 hrs — useful as a targeted "dinner pill." Have the patient take it 30 min before going to a restaurant so they eat less at the meal.
  • Topiramate at 25 mg at dinner specifically reduces PM cravings. The somnolence side effect is a feature (not a bug) when dosed at night.
  • Naltrexone/bupropion dosing: taking the second dose at 5 PM (not with dinner) reduces insomnia and helps with evening cravings.
  • Building naltrexone onto bupropion gradually (12.5 mg AM → 12.5 mg PM → 25 mg AM) dramatically reduces nausea vs starting full Contrave dosing immediately.
  • Orlistat niche use: counteracts constipation caused by GLP-1 RAs when fiber or polyethylene glycol isn't sufficient. Opposing GI effects can balance each other.
  • Plenity (cellulose-citric acid hydrogel) pearl: non-systemic, premeal option for BMI 25-40 that increases satiety. Best fit is a patient who wants a non-hormonal tool, can reliably drink water before meals, and understands that expected weight loss is modest compared with AOMs.
  • GLP-1 RA nausea is most prominent during the first 16 weeks of dose escalation, then plateaus. Counsel patients upfront — tolerability improves significantly after titration.
  • No need to routinely check calcitonin before starting GLP-1 RAs. Take careful personal/family history for MTC/MEN2 and work up thyroid nodules found on exam.
  • Tirzepatide and semaglutide slow gastric emptying → decreased oral contraceptive absorption. Counsel reproductive-age women to use non-oral or backup contraception.
  • With highly effective AOMs, monitor for excessive weight loss: minimum floor ~1,000 kcal/day and ≥65 g protein/day. Monitor body composition — sarcopenia is a real risk. Prescribe resistance exercise alongside medication.
  • STEP 2 key teaching: weight loss (not sema dose) drives HbA1c and CVD risk improvement. Higher dose produced more weight loss → more metabolic benefit. It's the weight loss doing the work.
  • Compounded semaglutide/tirzepatide: OMA position — not FDA-approved, purity/potency not assured. If prescribing, use only pharmacies with documented regulatory-compliant manufacturing and disclosed sourcing.
  • Supplements are not FDA-approved like drugs, cannot legitimately claim to treat disease, and many have limited or inconsistent weight-loss evidence. The algorithm specifically warns that herbal and dietary supplements are a common cause of hepatotoxicity.
  • Pediatric prescribing: semaglutide and liraglutide approved ≥12 years. Phentermine/topiramate approved ≥12 years. Orlistat approved ≥8 years. AAP recommends early treatment, not watchful waiting.
  • Asian patients: consider AOM treatment at BMI ≥25 (not ≥30). Visceral fat is disproportionately elevated at lower BMI. Use waist circumference as a key trigger (men ≥85–90 cm, women ≥80 cm per population-specific guidelines).
  • 2025 algorithm update topics worth keeping in your differential and counseling workflow: obesity in organ transplantation, nephrolithiasis risk with weight-loss therapies or high-oxalate states, and orthopedic complications that may improve with relatively modest weight loss.

How this lecture frames obesity

Core concept
Obesity is a complex, inflammatory disease with interplay between genes and environment. The lecture emphasizes that body weight is regulated, not simply volitionally controlled.
Clinical implication
“Physiology drives behavior, not the other way around.” That framing supports early, intensive, comprehensive treatment rather than blame-based counseling.

What adiposopathy means

Definition
The algorithm frames adiposopathy as “sick fat disease”: abnormal endocrine and immune responses from dysfunctional adipose tissue that promote metabolic disease.
Key idea
Metabolic health depends on crosstalk between adipose tissue and other organs including liver, muscle, pancreas, immune system, heart/vasculature, brain, endocrine glands, and intestine.

Common manifestations listed in the PDF

Insulin resistance / T2DM Hypertension Metabolic syndrome Atherogenic dyslipidemia Cardiovascular disease MASLD Hyperuricemia / gout PCOS / hypogonadism Nephrolithiasis Depression / neuropsychiatric effects

Energy expenditure basics

Main components
Total energy expenditure is made up of resting metabolic rate (RMR), physical activity, and diet-induced thermogenesis. For most people, RMR accounts for about 70% of total daily expenditure.
Why “easy maintenance” differs between people
The PDF emphasizes that NEAT can vary widely, roughly 150–500 kcal/day, and may explain why some people appear to maintain weight more easily despite similar formal exercise.

Practical metabolic adaptation points

ConceptClinical takeaway
RMRDriven largely by fat-free mass and organ activity; tends to be higher with more muscle, larger body size, younger age, and male sex.
Negative energy balanceThe common “3500 kcal per pound” rule is only a rough estimate because dynamic adaptations occur during weight loss.
Activity compensationFormal exercise is only one part of expenditure. Changes in NEAT and efficiency can meaningfully shift actual weight response.
Resistance trainingHelpful during caloric deficit because it can reduce muscle loss and blunt reductions in resting metabolic rate.
The algorithm points to indirect calorimetry, wearable devices, and energy-expenditure equations as tools to understand why progress may plateau even when a patient feels like they are “doing the same thing.”

What is body composition?

Body composition divides total body mass into fat mass (FM) and fat-free mass (FFM). FFM includes muscles, bones, organs, ligaments, tendons, and water. Skeletal muscle mass (SMM) is a subset of FFM. The clinical goal of obesity treatment is loss of excess fat mass with preservation of lean mass — not simply total weight reduction.

DXA — gold standard details

How it works
Low-radiation X-ray beams pass through tissues. Different tissues absorb different amounts of energy, allowing determination of tissue type and quantity. Outputs: % body fat, fat mass, lean mass, visceral fat (on capable machines), bone mineral density.
Key limitations for practice
• Not all machines distinguish visceral vs subcutaneous fat
• Not all machines accommodate very high body weight
• Accuracy requires: same machine each visit, standard SOP, routine calibration
• Percentile norms from 1999–2004 NHANES — underestimates severity vs current population
• Requires trained technician; often at an outside facility
• Assumptions (fixed water content in FFM) can cause inaccuracies

BIA — most practical for in-clinic use

How it works
Electrical current passes through body tissues. Muscle and water conduct easily; fat resists. Outputs vary by device. Types: hand-to-hand (handheld), foot-to-foot (scale), hand-to-foot (InBody©, Seca©). Multi-frequency BIA can measure both intracellular and extracellular water — more accurate.
Pre-test protocol (critical for reproducibility)
• Fast 8–12 hours before
• No drinking 2 hours prior
• No physical activity 24 hours prior
• Measure in morning or same time of day each visit
• No skin-to-skin contact during measurement
Devices without waist circumference input use less accurate population estimates for visceral adipose tissue (VAT)
Consumer BIA scales tested (Johnson et al.): Omron HBF-516 ($80), Tanita BC-568 InnerScan ($220), Tanita UM-081 ($49), InBody H2ON ($349), Withings Body Cardio ($165). Reproducibility varies significantly. Multi-frequency hand-to-foot devices (InBody, Seca) are more reliable for clinical tracking. Consistency of conditions matters more than which device you use.

Weight loss composition — what to expect and what to prevent

Expected: 25–30% of weight lost comes from fat-free mass, while 70–75% comes from fat mass. This is the typical ratio without active intervention to protect lean mass.
Muscle loss carries a metabolic cost: resting energy expenditure drops ~13 kcal/day per kg of muscle lost, vs only ~4.5 kcal/day per kg of fat lost. Losing muscle accelerates metabolic adaptation and weight regain.

Sarcopenia — definition, identification, and clinical management

Sarcopenia definition (EWGSOP): Low skeletal muscle mass + low muscle strength (e.g., handgrip) OR low muscle performance (e.g., walking speed). When all three are present = severe sarcopenia. Begins as early as the 4th decade; up to 50% of muscle mass may be lost by the 8th decade.

Core takeaways

Calorie targets
Low-calorie dietary patterns are roughly 1,000–1,200 kcal/day for women and 1,200–1,600 kcal/day for men. Very-low-calorie diets are generally <800 kcal/day and should be medically supervised.
What improves outcomes
Nutrition therapy works best when it is evidence-based, quantitative, qualitative, individualized, and realistic enough for the patient to follow consistently.

What to prescribe, not just what to avoid

Protein
Most adults will do better when each meal is built around a clear protein source. The algorithm gives a broad protein range of 0.8-2.0 g/kg/day; older adults, active patients, and people at risk for lean-mass loss often need the higher end.
Fiber
Aim for at least 25-30 g/day from vegetables, fruit, beans, lentils, high-fiber grains, nuts, and seeds. Fiber improves satiety, bowel regularity, and glycemic response.
Meal structure
A simple repeatable structure often works better than free-form calorie advice: 2-3 planned meals, protein first, produce at most meals, and a defined snack plan when needed.

High-yield counseling points

Diet quality
Favor whole foods over ultra-processed foods, complex carbs over refined sugars, whole fruit over juice/smoothies, and minimize trans fats, sugary beverages, and excess sodium.
Fiber and protein
The PDF targets at least 25–30 g/day of fiber for adults and a broad protein range of 0.8–2.0 g/kg/day depending on age, activity, and health status.

High-yield troubleshooting

If the patient says "I do well all day and overeat at night"
Look for under-eating earlier in the day, long gaps without protein, decision fatigue, and reward eating. A structured lunch and planned late-afternoon snack often help more than telling the patient to use willpower at 9 pm.
If the patient says "I am not losing even though I eat healthy"
Check portions, hidden liquid calories, calorie-dense "healthy" foods, restaurant meals, weekend drift, alcohol, and whether intake is objectively different on workdays vs off days.
If the patient says "I am never hungry on my GLP-1"
That can be good for intake control, but it can also create low protein, dehydration, constipation, fatigue, and lean-mass loss. Counsel on minimum intake quality, not just appetite suppression.
If constipation shows up
Reassess fluid intake, fiber dose, total intake, physical activity, and whether the patient rapidly increased fiber without enough hydration. The answer is usually not "stop eating vegetables."

Prevent undernutrition

Smaller portions, slower eating, separating eating from drinking when helpful, and limiting greasy or overly sweet foods are practical ways to reduce nausea and early satiety problems.

NF4 — Carbohydrates: Choose quality, not zero

Choose these carbs more often

  • Non-starchy vegetables (eat freely): greens, broccoli, cauliflower, peppers, zucchini, cucumber, asparagus, mushrooms, tomatoes, cabbage
  • Fruit: berries, apples, pears, oranges, peaches, melon — whole fruit is far better than juice
  • Legumes: black beans, lentils, chickpeas, edamame — high in fiber AND protein
  • Whole grains: oats, quinoa, brown rice, farro, whole-grain bread, high-fiber tortillas
  • Root vegetables: sweet potato, regular potato, carrots — filling and nutrient-rich

Limit these carbs

  • Sugary beverages: soda, juice, sweet tea, lemonade, sports drinks, sweet coffee drinks (biggest source of hidden calories for most people)
  • Ultra-processed snacks: chips, crackers, cookies, candy — engineered to be hard to stop eating
  • Refined bread and white carbs: white bread, bagels, white rice in large amounts, pastries
  • Alcohol: adds calories quickly, lowers inhibitions around food choices, and disrupts sleep
You do not have to eliminate carbs. The goal is to shift toward carbs that come with fiber, protein, and nutrients — and away from ones that spike blood sugar and are easy to overeat.

Habits that help you eat less without trying

  • Eat slowly — it takes 15–20 minutes for fullness signals to reach your brain. Put the fork down between bites.
  • Use a smaller plate — 9-inch plates instead of 12-inch plates can reduce portion size naturally.

QA11 — Common questions answered

QuestionAnswer
Do I have to count calories?Not necessarily. Many people do well by focusing on protein, meal structure, and beverages. If you plateau, tracking for 2–4 weeks can reveal hidden calorie sources. Apps like Cronometer or MyFitnessPal make this easy.
Should I cut out all carbs?No. The goal is to shift which carbs you eat — toward high-fiber, nutrient-dense ones — not to eliminate carbs entirely. Vegetables, fruit, beans, and oats are all carbs and all support weight loss.
Are artificial sweeteners okay?In moderation, they are a reasonable tool for reducing sugar intake. Sparkling water, diet drinks, and zero-calorie sweeteners are preferable to sugary alternatives for most people.
What if I'm not hungry? (especially on GLP-1 meds)Eat on a schedule regardless of hunger. Even a small protein-focused meal protects your muscle and keeps your metabolism stable. Skipping meals can lead to muscle loss over time.
Can I eat fruit if I'm trying to lose weight?Yes. Whole fruit is filling, fibrous, and nutritious. Berries, apples, pears, and citrus are excellent choices. Avoid juice — it removes the fiber and concentrates the sugar.
What's the best diet for weight loss?The best diet is the one you can actually follow consistently. Mediterranean, lower-carb, protein-forward, and structured meal-replacement plans all work — what they have in common is creating a calorie deficit through a structure you repeat.
How long until I see results?Most people notice 2–4 lbs in the first 2 weeks (some fluid + early fat), then 0.5–1.5 lbs/week with a consistent plan. With medication, weight loss may be faster. Progress is not linear — expect some weeks with no change.
What if I hit a plateau?Plateaus are normal. Before changing anything drastic: audit beverages, re-check protein intake, confirm you're not underreporting restaurant meals, and make sure strength training is in the plan. Sometimes the plan is right and the body just needs more time.
Can I have a "cheat day"?A planned off-plan meal occasionally is fine. A full cheat day can erase a week's progress. Instead, plan 1–2 flex meals per week where you enjoy something different — without calling them failures.

SP12 — Special situations

On GLP-1 or tirzepatide (Wegovy, Zepbound, Saxenda)

  • Eat smaller meals — stop at "comfortably satisfied," not stuffed.
  • Prioritize protein even when appetite is very low — muscle loss is real without it.
  • Avoid high-fat or very sweet foods during dose increases — they worsen nausea.
  • Eat slowly and chew thoroughly — gastric emptying is slowed on these medications.
  • Drink fluids between meals, not during, if bloating is a problem.

If you have diabetes or prediabetes

  • Lower-carbohydrate eating often improves blood sugar faster than any other dietary change.
  • Eat carbs with protein and fiber to blunt blood sugar spikes.
  • Watch for low blood sugar if on insulin or a sulfonylurea — your medication dose may need to be reduced as you eat less.
  • Check blood sugar more frequently when changing your diet significantly.

  • You are unable to eat enough protein or drink enough fluids for more than 2–3 days.
  • You feel weak, dizzy, or faint — especially on medication.
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation, or reflux is preventing you from following the plan.
  • You need a more specific plan: diabetes, kidney disease, bariatric history, food allergies, or eating disorder history.

Orlistat (Xenical / Alli) — Patient Guide

What you need to know about your weight-loss medication

Medication Guide
What it is: Orlistat works in your intestine — not your brain. It blocks about one-third of the fat you eat from being absorbed by your body. The unabsorbed fat passes out of your body instead of being stored. It does not suppress appetite.

RXHow to take it

  • Take with each main meal or within 1 hour after the meal.
  • If you skip a meal or your meal has no fat — skip the dose.
  • Prescription: 120 mg (Xenical) — 3 times per day with meals.
  • OTC version: 60 mg (Alli) — 3 times per day with meals.
  • Take your daily multivitamin at a different time — at least 2 hours before or after orlistat.

NFYour fat limit per meal

  • Keep dietary fat to less than 30% of calories per meal (roughly 15 g fat or less per meal).
  • Read nutrition labels — look at "Total Fat" grams per serving.
  • This is not just a recommendation — it directly reduces GI side effects.
Understanding the GI side effects: Oily stools, urgent bowel movements, or oily spotting are NOT traditional drug side effects — they are your body responding to fat you ate. They get worse if you eat high-fat meals. Think of them as a built-in feedback signal. Keeping fat low at each meal prevents most of these issues.

  • Orlistat reduces absorption of fat-soluble vitamins: A, D, E, and K.
  • Take a daily multivitamin that includes these vitamins — at a different time from your orlistat dose.
  • Your provider may check your Vitamin D level at follow-up.

MDCall the clinic if you have

  • Severe kidney pain or blood in urine — possible kidney stone.

Definition
Motivational interviewing is a collaborative, patient-centered, goal-directed counseling approach that guides behavior change by drawing out the patient’s own motivations and commitment.
Core spirit
The PDF emphasizes partnership, evocation, acceptance, and compassion, with focus on change talk rather than sustain talk.

4 processes of motivational interviewing

1. Engaging
Build rapport and make the patient feel heard rather than managed.
2. Focusing
Agree on the problem worth working on right now.
3. Evoking
Draw out reasons for change from the patient, not from a lecture.
4. Planning
Turn readiness into a concrete next step.

The algorithm also highlights the 5 A’s model of behavior change: Ask, Assess, Advise, Agree, Arrange/Assist.
High-yield behavioral pearl from the PDF: avoid arguments. When resistance shows up, roll with it, use reflections, and explore discrepancy rather than confronting the patient head-on.

Why this matters

Higher risk group
Adolescents and young adults with neurodiversity have higher lifetime obesity risk than neurotypical peers. The lecture cites especially high obesity prevalence in autism and elevated rates across ADHD, intellectual disability, and learning/developmental conditions.
Mechanisms
Neurodiversity can interact with appetite through altered hunger/satiety perception, reward-circuit activation, executive dysfunction, impulsivity, food aversions, sleep disruption, and medication effects.

Psychotropic and related medication issues

High-yield offenders
Second-generation antipsychotics, SSRIs, clonidine, tricyclic antidepressants, and mood stabilizers can all push weight up in this population.
Useful mitigation strategy
The lecture specifically highlights metformin as an off-label tool that can attenuate antipsychotic-associated weight gain and insulin resistance, especially when started early.
Neurodiversity pearl: intuitive eating may not work the same way in patients with impaired interoception. The lecture suggests many neurodiverse patients may do better with more structured or “mechanical” eating patterns than with cue-only approaches.

Why sleep belongs in every obesity visit

Sleep-obesity cycle
The PDF describes a bidirectional relationship: obesity can worsen sleep disorders, and sleep disorders can create metabolic consequences that contribute to obesity. Sleep deprivation lowers leptin, raises ghrelin, increases snacking, and can reduce fat loss during a hypocaloric diet.
Screening tools in the PDF
Sleep diary, Epworth Sleepiness Scale, and STOP-BANG are specifically listed as screening tools.

What changes in midlife

Weight vs body composition
The lecture emphasizes that aging drives much of the weight gain, while menopause more strongly drives body composition change, especially central fat deposition and lean-mass loss.
Typical pattern
Midlife weight gain often occurs at about 0.4–0.7 kg per year, with waist circumference rising and adiposity redistributing centrally during the menopause transition.

Why it matters clinically

AreaLecture takeaway
Cardiovascular diseaseMenopause is a major independent risk factor for CVD, and central obesity amplifies CVD mortality risk.
Hypertension / metabolic diseaseEach 10 kg increase in weight raises systolic and diastolic blood pressure; dysglycemia and dyslipidemia accelerate in midlife.
MASLDThe prevalence of MASLD rises substantially after age 50, and menopause is a major risk factor.
OSA and osteoarthritisMechanical complications increase during midlife as adiposity rises and redistributes.
Cancer riskMidlife weight gain and central obesity are tied to higher obesity-related cancer risk, including endometrial, ovarian, breast, and colorectal cancers.

Management themes from the lecture

Prevention approach
Counsel proactively about expected weight and body-composition changes. Dietary changes with or without behavior modification and physical activity outperform physical activity alone for weight-gain prevention.
Symptom management
Hormone therapy may reduce visceral adiposity, total adiposity, and muscle-mass loss when appropriate. If hormone therapy is contraindicated, the lecture highlights nonhormonal symptom strategies and careful medication selection.
Medication pearl from the lecture: when choosing nonhormonal menopause therapies in patients with obesity, avoid weight-positive options when possible. The speaker specifically flags gabapentin, paroxetine, citalopram, escitalopram, and clonidine as less favorable from a weight perspective, while mentioning venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, oxybutynin, and fezolinetant as options to consider.
